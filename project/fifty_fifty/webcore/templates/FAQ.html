FAQs

  • How often should I meet up with my mentor?

    Once a fortnight.

  • How do I prepare for my first meeting with my mentee/mentor?

    Think about what you would like to achieve out of the program, what you can offer to them, set expectations from the start and also set some SMART ( (S)pecific (M)easurable (A)greed-upon (R)ealistic (T)ime-based) goals for yourself.

  • How should I prepare for my meetings with my mentee/mentor?

    Think about what you would talk to them about, and go over the fortnightly content posted and also have a think about some questions you might want to ask them.

  • How should we conduct meetings?

    You could grab a coffee, pick a quiet spot to talk, or go for a walk! Whatever suits you!

  • I am having an issue with my mentee/mentor - who do I talk to about this?

    Contact Fifty50’s mentoring team at mentoring@fifty50.org.au, or click the Contact Us link in the navigation bar to fill in a form online.

  • I haven’t heard from my mentee/mentor yet - who do I talk to about this?

    Contact Fifty50’s mentoring team at mentoring@fifty50.org.au, or click the Contact Us link in the navigation bar to fill in a form online.

  • Where can I find personal development resources for myself and my mentee?

    Fifty50’s Career Development Program conducts fortnightly workshops run by the Department of Defence. You can also check out the Resources page for additional resources about gender equity, university life, services on campus, and mental health.

  • What if I’m uncomfortable with some of the content material?

    The content is just there for your personal development. If there is any content that you feel uncomfortable with, or if you would just like to discuss other aspects of your personal life, feel free to discuss this with your mentor.

  • How can I be a good mentor?

    • - Practice active listening. Paraphrasing is a good tool to demonstrate you are listening.

    • - Choose a time of day when you’re not too busy so you can give them full attention.

    • - Perhaps choose a quieter area or go for a walk. Walking and holding a hot drink is a great way to see the campus and give them a chance to open up. Sitting next to each other is also better than sitting across from each other.

    • - Set some mutual expectations from the start – ask them what they want to get out of the program, have a think about what you can offer, set a regular meeting time and method for communication etc.

    • - Don’t rescue them from their problems – give them tools to solve them themselves. Offer advice, experience, ideas, etc. It’s better to be a peer than a parent figure.

    • - If you’re not comfortable or it becomes too serious, you can change the topic. Setting some ground rules at the start can help you avoid some tricky situations.

    • - If they’re talking about suicide, self-harm or harm to others then refer them on. The ANU Counselling Centre is a great place for both them and you to utilise. They have phone or drop in sessions in the morning.

    • - Other services on campus you might refer them to:

      • Academic Skills and Learning Centre (great for academic support – you should look into this for yourself too. Remember you aren’t a private tutor, so if they are asking for specific help, refer them on or encourage them to use their peer networks).

      • ANUSA (for financial or legal support)

      • Student Services (for academic advice, enrollment problems, etc)
